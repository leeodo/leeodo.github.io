Abstract

Washington, DC has had higher violent crime rates, when compared to neighboring states, Maryland and Virginia. As of recently, 2021 wraps up as one of the DC region’s most violent years in ages. Crime rates in Washington, DC vary by ward, where particular wards are impacted at a higher rate than others. Increasing violent crime rates harm the overall wellness of individuals and communities. Understanding what factors lead to the increase in violent crime it may be possible to reduce their frequency. To address this challenge, we propose building a data visualization that allows city administrators to visually explore a dataset of violent crimes committed by types of neighborhoods and explore what demographical, economical, and sociological factors are commonly associated with wards with those who experience higher rates of violent crime. This app will show the distribution of factors contributing to crimes committed and allow users to explore different aspects of this data by filtering and re-ordering different variables to compare factors that contribute to neighborhoods/ areas with high crime rates. We will focus on the different wards of Washington DC and will use some cities and counties in Maryland and Virginia.

Introduction

Crime is a significant concern for all, including city officials, businesses, everyday citizens, and other stakeholders. The prevalence of crime in a city often results in negative economic, political, environmental, and societal consequences that have lasting effects. As students living in Washington, DC, we're curious about crime rates in the newest city we reside in, Washington, DC. Using data from various datasets provided by the Washington, DC Metropolitan Police Department and multiple datasets on the OpenData DC website to better understand crime rates in Washington, DC, and neighboring states.


In general, data visualizations of crime are helpful for all stakeholders to predict crime risk, improve community relations, form budgets, allocate resources, and more. We believe that different groups will benefit from our data visualization. For example, those new to Washington, DC, will be able to use our visualizations to make informed decisions on their housing and ensure their safety. Similarly, business owners may be able to use our data visualization to better equip their business with better protective measures, such as investing in better insurance for their business. Our data visualizations are also helpful for policymakers within the Washington, DC government, who aim to decrease crime for the health and safety of community members. By providing different economic, social, and demographic characteristics of neighborhoods with high and low crime rates, we hope to highlight variables that directly or indirectly influence crime rates.


The project's main objective is to answer the following questions 1) what do crime rates look like in Washington, DC when compared to neighboring states, Maryland and Virginia throughout time? 2) What are the crime rates in Washington, DC? How do crime rates vary across neighborhoods and different wards in the city? 3) What are the most common types of crimes in Washington, DC? 4) What social, economic, and political characteristics correlate with the most common type of crime in Washington, DC?


To answer the questions listed above, our team used various interactive and non-interactive visualizations that city members, businesses, community members, and other stakeholders can utilize. We created visualizations assuming that our audience is groups who take a glance at data and do not have a technical background.

Data

Data Description

We used the following datasets to answer our questions: State Crime, DC crime dataset, demographic characteristics of DC by ward, economic characteristics of DC by ward, social characteristics of DC by ward, and housing characteristics of DC by ward. The description of each data is as follows:


  • State Crime: Based on the Unified Crime Reporting Statistics and the collaboration of the U.S. Department of Justice and FBI, this dataset provides the crime rates and totals for states across the U.S. from 1960 to 2019. The crime reports are categorized into property (Burglary, Larceny, Motor) and violent crime (Assault, Murder, Rape, Robbery). We will use the subset containing only the information about the DMV area, partially as a reference for the other datasets.
  • DC Crime dataset is an incident-based dataset. Every row is an incident that contains detailed time elements, geographic elements, and crime category elements. This data is made public by the Washington, DC Metropolitan police department. The whole dataset includes data as early as eight years ago. So it’s a very comprehensive dataset.

  • Demographic characteristics of DC by ward contains variables age, sex, race, ethnicity, total housing units, and voting age population by ward. This is a tidy data pulled from American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year data for 2020.

  • Economic characteristics of DC by ward contain variables to measure employment, commuting, occupation, income, health insurance, poverty, and more. This is a tidy data pulled from American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year data for 2020.

  • Social characteristics of DC by ward contains variables on household type, education, disability, language, computer/internet use, and more. This is a tidy data pulled from American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year data for 2020.

  • Housing characteristics of DC by ward contain variables on occupancy status, Units, Rooms, Year built, Owner/Renter (Tenure), Mortgage/Rent costs, and more. This is a tidy data pulled from American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year data for 2020.

DMV CRIMES CONDITION

Q1: What do crime rates look like in Washington, DC when compared to neighboring states Maryland and Virginia throughout time?

Figure-1: Crime rates in Washington, DC, Maryland, & Virginia 1960-2019
Method: The time series plot above displays total crime rates from 1960 to 2019 in Washington, DC, Virginia, and Maryland, also known as the DMV. This plot was created in R using the plotly package. Readers can interact with the plot by clicking on the graph to focus on particular time periods or states. The colors, red, yellow, and blue are representing the main colors of Washington, DC, Maryland, and Virginia state flags.

Washington, DC, significantly has the highest rate of crime when compared to neighboring states, Maryland and Virginia. Compared to Maryland and Virginia, the crime rates in Washington, DC have had much more fluctuation throughout time. As seen in Figure-1, while crime rates were fairly low in 1960 in Washington, DC, Maryland, and Virginia, Washington, DC’s crime rate spiked in the 1970s, while crime rates in Virginia and Maryland remained relatively low. We see a similar spike in the late 1970s, followed by a sharp decline in the early 1980s. We see the same sharp rise and a sharp decline from the mid - 1980s to the 1990s. Crime rates for Washington, DC, Maryland, and Virginia had a sharp decline since the mid-1990, most likely as a result of the Violent Crime Control Act and Law Enforcement Act of 1994 passed by president Bill Clinton. Despite the decrease in crime most likely triggered by the crime bill, Washington, DC continues to have higher crime rates than neighboring states Maryland and Virginia.

DC CRIMES IN 2020

Q3: What happened to the crime situation in DC in 2020? What are the crime rates in Washington, DC? How do crime rates vary across neighborhoods and different wards in the city?

The spike of crime incidents in June 2020 caught our attention, we then wandered about the geospatial distributions of the crimes in DC and mapped several heatmaps to answer our data science questions.


Figure-3: Marker Cluster 2020
Method: This visualization is meant to show a more detailed distribution of crimes that happened in DC. This used the same data as the overall crime choropleth since it contains longitude and latitude. The markers are added one by one and the leaflet package is smart enough to aggregate markers based on location. The map has many circles zooming in and out. The number on the circle represents the number of crimes happened in the area. When users hover their mouse on a circle, it will show a polygon corresponding to the area the specific circle represents. Furthermore, the color of the circle is a gradient of numbers as green is low and orange is high.
Innovative: This marker cluster map replaces scatter plots on a map. A scatter plot would be sufficient if there are fewer dots on a map because the audience can see the density and geometry information wouldn’t be blocked. The marker cluster makes the map less “busy” so that the audience can see the geometry information on a map. If an audience wants to see the detailed location information of each incident, an audience just needs to scroll up and the map would zoom in and show individual incidents.

As shown in Figure-3, there were a total of 27,932 crimes in Washington, DC in 2020. When zoomed in, the crime count is divided to the east of the Anacostia River and west of the Anacostia River at 755 and 27,177 crime counts, respectively. When we continue to zoom in we're able to see crime counts by neighborhood and even down to crime counts by streets. Using the information gained from Figure-2, individuals, businesses, and city officials are able to understand crime occurrences better.
For example, imagen a potential student deciding between attending American University, Howard University, Georgetown University, and George Washington University. This particular student factors in crime occurrence as an important deciding factor and therefore reviews Figure-3. This student can zoom into each school's neighborhoods to gain information on crime occurrences. We see that the Georgetown area has had 585 total crimes and Foggy Bottom, where George Washington is located, had 104 crimes in 2020. American University Park and neighborhoods surrounding Howard University each had 112 crimes in 2020. Based on information gained from Figure-3, Georgetown Washington University may be more attractive to the student due to the lower count of crime in the foggy bottom neighborhood.

Figure-5: Washington, DC crime by Wards
Method: This choropleth is meant to show the difference in violent crime situations across eight wards in Washington, D.C. The data was first filtered by crime type to pick out violent crimes only then aggregated to the total number of crimes that happened in 2020 per ward. Then the crime rate was derived. Lastly, the aggregated data has been merged with the geojson file. The color of each ward is based on the absolute value of crimes. A legend of colormap is provided for reference. This map has a hover feature. When users hover their mouse over the map, users can see specific details of crime statistics.

The District of Columbia is divided into eight wards. Each ward has approximately 75,000 residents, each with its rich history, vibrant neighborhoods, and a diverse population. As shown in Figure-5, these wards have different characteristics, including crime counts. Wards 2, Ward 5, and Ward 7 had the highest crime counts at 4684, 4394, and 4194, respectively. Ward 3 significantly has the lowest crime count of 1710. Figure-5 is particularly useful for Advisory Neighborhood Commissions.
Each ward is assigned George Floyd protests in Washington DC. ANCs are elected volunteers who serve two-year terms. The ANCs were established to bridge the gap between the government and the people. There are now 40 ANCs, with each ANC area subdivided into many smaller neighborhoods. Since only one Commissioner is elected per district, they are called Single Member Districts (SMDs). The ANCs may also initiate recommendations for improving city services, conduct neighborhood improvement programs, and monitor resident complaints. ANCs can view figures to gain information on crime counts in their respective wards.

• Housing Characteristics

Figure-11: Housing Conditions across the Eight DC wards
Method: The housing dataset contains three main features: number of bedrooms, monthly rent, and the percentage of rent over the owner's income, and each of them has several categories. A stacked bar chart would be a perfect option to show both the total number of households in each ward and the specific structures of these households (represented by the colored stacks). The tooltips will show the name of that category (e.g. 1 bedroom, 35%-39.9%), the number of households in that category, and the ward number. A dropdown menu with the function of selecting different variables (features) is incorporated, enabling the viewer to see structural differences between the features other than only making comparisons between wards. Another exciting feature of the plot is that viewers can select the categories in the legend and make further exploration of the housing condition without destroying the format of the bar chart. A new bar chart will form instead of losing certain segments of the bars.
Innovative: It takes many lines to plot and reveals insights that would be ignored.


Now, let's take a look at the housing conditions in DC. In any ward, only a few households have more than 3 bedrooms. But in particular, Ward 2, being the most dangerous ward in DC in terms of crime incidents, has both the largest number and largest fraction of households having 0 or 1 bedroom. Ward 2 also has a high fraction of people paying rent over $2000. However, when it comes to the rent/income percentage, all the wards share a similar structure. Based on the plot, we could find some traces of correlation between housing conditions and crime incidents but it's far from drawing some valid conclusions.

Discussion

We also find social and economic factors impact the occurrence of violent crime. Our key takeaways are as follows.



As shown in Figure-3, Figure-4, and Figure-5 crime rates in DC vary by ward, neighborhood, and street of DC.

- Takeaway 3
